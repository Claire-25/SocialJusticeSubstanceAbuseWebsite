Drugs

The Reality

Addiction is a chronic disease characterized by drug seeking and use that is compulsive, or difficult to control, despite harmful consequences. The initial decision to take drugs is voluntary for most people, but repeated drug use can lead to brain changes that challenge an addicted person’s self-control and interfere with their ability to resist intense urges to take drugs. These brain changes can be persistent, which is why drug addiction is considered a "relapsing" disease—people in recovery from drug use disorders are at increased risk for returning to drug use even after years of not taking the drug. Drug consumption can lead to dependency, liver disease, and impaired judgment.

The Ideal

People don't take drugs unless medically necessary.

The Bridge

Education, awareness, and support systems help mitigate risks.

Reality of Substance Abuse

What is Substance Abuse?

Substance Abuse is the excessive use of psychoactive drugs, such as alcohol, pain medications, or illegal drugs. It can lead to physical, social, or emotional harm.

Who or What Does Substance Abuse Affect?

Because harmful substances have devastating effects on the user, many may not take into consideration the other people involved, but addiction can affect the family in many ways. Studies show that growing up with an alcoholic parent can affect a child, and spouses and parents can be similarly impacted by addiction.

What are the Causes?

The History

7,000-5,000 BCE: Grapes cultivated for wine in what is now modern-day Georgia

3,400 BCE: The opium poppy is cultivated in lower Mesopotamia. Sumerians refer to it as the "joy plant"

12th century BCE: Early references to "drink madness" from ancient Egypt and Greece

1st Century AD: The Roman philosopher Seneca defines drunkenness as a type of insanity

4th Century AD: St. John Chrysostom compares inebriation to other known diseases. He is, however, in favor of using wine due to the sacramental role of the Eucharist

The History

The Middle Ages: Attitudes towards drunkenness varied. Some religious sects prohibited the use of substances, while others permitted or encouraged it (Benedictine and Franciscan monks)

1560s-90s: There was an early description of narcotic withdrawal, cravings, and compulsions due to opium traffic. "If they leave off somwhat taking it, then they feel physically ill."

1789: First American temperance society organized in Litchfield, Connecticut

1799: China's emperor, Kia King, bans opium, making trade and poppy cultivation illegal

1825-1850: Temperance movement sweeps America and embraces total abstinence as a goal
